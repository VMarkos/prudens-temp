Othello's Language

Shakespear's Othello is probably one of his most distinguished works in terms of linguistic beauty. While not claiming to match the Shakespearean language of Othello, Othello's language claims to be expressive enough to let you coach a machine play the game at an adequate level. Namely, its language provides three built-in predicates:

  • cell(Row, Col, Content), corresponding to a single cell at row Row and column Col, while Content indicates whether the cell is blank (0), or contains a black or white piece (-1 and 1 respectively).
  • legalMove(Row, Col) corresponds to cells where the player's pieces may be put legally, according to Othello's rules;
  • move(Row, Col) corresponds to a move action and should be used in order to indicate preference regarding putting a piece to a certain cell or not.

In all the above, rows and columns are counted from top to bottom and from left to right, starting from zero.

A Simple Example

A very simple policy could be the following one: 

							@KnowledgeBase
                                R1 :: legalMove(X, Y) implies move(X, Y);

That is, any cell that is legal to put a piece to should be considered. Evidently, the above is not a sustainable strategy, since it is actually equivalent to playing at random. So, on top of that, we may add some more sophisticated heuristics: 

							@KnowledgeBase
                                R1 :: legalMove(X, Y) implies move(X, Y);
                                R2 :: legalMove(0, 0), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(X, 0) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R3 :: legalMove(0, 0), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(Y, 0) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R4 :: legalMove(0, 0) implies move(0, 0);
                                R5 :: legalMove(0, 7), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(X, 0) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R6 :: legalMove(0, 7), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(Y, 7) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R7 :: legalMove(0, 7) implies move(0, 7);
                                R8 :: legalMove(7, 0), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(X, 7) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R9 :: legalMove(7, 0), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(Y, 0) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R10 :: legalMove(7, 0) implies move(7, 0);
                                R11 :: legalMove(7, 7), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(X, 7) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R12 :: legalMove(7, 7), legalMove(X, Y), -?=(Y, 7) implies -move(X, Y);
                                R13 :: legalMove(7, 7) implies move(7, 7);

While somewhat cryptic, the above encodes in Prudens's language the following simple statement: "In case you can play to one of the corners of the board, do it!". Indeed, if you paste the above policy on the policy editor, you will observe that, as playing, the machine (always playing black) chooses to play on a corner, whenever this is possible.
